Every great creator has a toolkit. These are the fundamental elements of film theory‚Äîthe language you'll use to tell your story, evoke emotion, and captivate your audience. Click on each card to learn more.

Shot Composition

How you frame your shots to guide the viewer's eye and tell your story visually.

Think about the Rule of Thirds, leading lines, and depth of field. Each choice helps build your narrative!

Lighting

Using light and shadow to set the mood, direct attention, and create atmosphere.

Whether it's bright and cheerful or dark and moody, your lighting choices convey a lot of information and emotion.

Sound Design

The art of using audio‚Äîdialogue, music, and effects‚Äîto enhance the experience.

Sound is a powerful tool! A great video often has great sound, so don't overlook its impact on your story.

Color Theory

Using colors to evoke emotions and create a cohesive visual style.

Colors can symbolize ideas, create contrast, and establish the overall tone of your film.

Editing

The process of assembling shots to create a seamless and compelling narrative.

Pacing, rhythm, and transitions are key. Good editing is invisible and serves the story.

Mise-en-Sc√®ne

Everything that appears in the frame: sets, props, costumes, and actors.

This French term means "placing on stage." It's about designing the world of your video.

Step 3: Crew Call üì£

On a big set, many people have specialized roles. For your project, you'll likely wear many of these hats yourself! Understanding these roles helps you plan all the tasks you'll need to tackle.

Director

The creative visionary who guides the actors and crew to bring the script to life.

Producer

The project manager who handles logistics, budget, and scheduling.

Cinematographer

The artist behind the camera, responsible for the visual look and feel.

Editor

The storyteller who assembles the footage in post-production to create the final film.
